# Mistral — Founder in Residence — Application Questions

## Tell us about something impressive that you've built

I joined Bazzile in 2020 — a real-estate technology startup — first as an
employee and then as a partner. I built the first production version of the
product alone, end-to-end: Elixir/Phoenix backend, Flutter mobile, PostgreSQL,
and infrastructure on Hetzner with container-based deployments via Kamal.

Once the product had traction, I hired two software engineers and one
Technical Success Manager, and kept that team together for multiple years.

Looking back, I thought my biggest achievement would be technical. It isn't.
The real one is the human side — a small team that chose to stay and ship
for years, and a network of customers, partners and early backers built on
the same kind of deep trust. The technology got us to market; the people
kept us there.

## How would you help accelerate the deployment of frontier AI within organizations?

The mistake I see most often is selling "AI" as the value. It isn't. The
value is the pain you remove or the friction you replace. Models are
commodities; the wedge is finding the right place to plant one inside
someone's existing workflow — and then making sure the human ends up
doing more of what they care about, not less.

In practice that means starting from the human, not from the platform.
At Wonderful, the deals I was able to open with private banks weren't
won by demoing platform capabilities. They were won by anchoring on a
single concrete workflow — for example, accelerating the generation of
an investment plan right after a banker's client meeting, so the
proposal is ready before the client even gets home. The banker sees
themselves doing more of what they care about (advising) and less of
what they don't (drafting). Selling a generic "AI platform" without
that anchor leaves people paralysed — they don't know what to ask for,
and the technology just sits there.

I'd accelerate Mistral's deployment by leaning into that posture. Show
up with use cases, not capabilities. Find the painful workflow — the
manual report, the lost search, the Tier-1 ticket queue, the compliance
review — wire a Mistral model into it, prove the lift, then expand.
Mistral is unusually well-placed for this: open weights, on-prem
deployment and frontier-grade quality mean we can put an agent exactly
where the data and the pain live, instead of asking the client to
compromise.

## What excites you about Mistral?

Three things.

The open weights, first. I've spent my career on open-source stacks — every
layer of Bazzile is open, and the rebuild of our voice agent runs on
Elixir/Jido and Voxtral precisely because I want to own the model the same
way I own the database. Mistral is one of the only frontier labs that lets
a builder do that today. That's not a marketing line for me; it's a
deal-breaker when I'm thinking about where to spend the next decade.

Second, the fact that it's European. I'm French, based in Geneva, and the
enterprises I've spent my career inside — banks, asset managers, industrial
groups in Romandie — care deeply about where their data sits and who they
depend on. Working with a frontier lab whose answer to "can we deploy
on-prem?" is "yes, by design" changes the conversation from the very first
meeting.

And the founders. Mistral has assembled one of the very few teams that
competes at the model frontier without burying the science or losing focus.
The technical bar speaks for itself; what excites me more is the clarity of
what they think AI in Europe should look like. That's the kind of room I
want to be in.
