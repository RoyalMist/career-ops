1. Describe your biggest achievement, at work or in life in general. Explain why.

My biggest achievement was rebuilding the entire technology department at FlowBank, a FINMA-regulated Swiss bank, essentially from scratch.

When I arrived, the previous IT department had been let go almost entirely — only three people remained. I had to rebuild a full organisation of 40 people while simultaneously keeping the bank operational and delivering on an ambitious transformation agenda.

I made a deliberate strategic choice: instead of competing with established Geneva banks for Java engineers — a battle we could not win on compensation alone — I built the new stack around Go. This gave us a genuine differentiator in the local talent market. Engineers who wanted something different, a modern language, a greenfield environment, a chance to shape things from day one — they came to us. Combined with competitive packages, this let us attract strong talent fast.

In parallel, I drove three critical workstreams: migrating the bank's infrastructure to AWS, delivering a new FINMA-compliant client onboarding system, and laying the architectural foundation for a proper core banking platform to replace the patchwork of legacy systems.

What makes this my proudest achievement is the sheer number of constraints I had to navigate simultaneously — regulatory pressure, near-zero starting headcount, an aggressive timeline, and the need to keep a live bank running throughout. It taught me that scaling an organisation is not just about hiring: it is about making deliberate technology and culture choices that compound — attracting the right people, giving them the right tools, and building something they are proud to work on.

2. Explain what "good software engineering" means for you in the context of a fast-scaling company.

Good software engineering is, first and foremost, software that addresses real user needs. In a fast-scaling company, the temptation is to ship everything — but the discipline is to ship what makes sense, validate it, and move on. Features that nobody uses are not just wasted effort; they are ongoing maintenance burden, cognitive load for the team, and surface area for bugs.

That is why good engineering also means having the confidence to remove things. Dead code, unused features, obsolete abstractions — they should be cut without fear. This confidence only comes from solid foundations: a codebase that is resilient, maintainable, and battle-tested.

For me, Test-Driven Development is central to this. TDD is not just a testing practice — it is a way of thinking. It embraces trial and error as a first-class development methodology: you define expected behaviour, write the simplest thing that works, refactor, and iterate. This cycle naturally produces software that is well-structured, safe to change, and easy to reason about. In a scaling context, where the codebase and the team grow together, this matters enormously — new engineers can contribute with confidence because the test suite tells them immediately when something breaks.

Today, AI is transforming how we write software, and good engineering practices matter even more in that context. AI-assisted development is powerful, but without strong methodologies — clear specifications, solid test coverage, disciplined review — it can amplify bad habits just as easily as good ones. The teams that will benefit most from AI are the ones that already have the foundations in place: well-tested code, clear ownership, and a culture where quality is not negotiable. AI accelerates the iteration loop; TDD and sound engineering ensure the loop converges toward the right outcome.
